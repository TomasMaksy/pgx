## The solution

**Eyebrow:** The solution

**Headline:** One Profile. A Lifetime of Smarter Prescribing.

**Intro:** Once a pharmacogenomic profile is generated, it can guide medication selection, dosing, and safety decisions whenever a relevant drug is prescribed.

### Step 01 — Sequence Once

A one-time sample is sequenced to identify the clinically actionable genetic variants that influence drug response. No repeat testing — the same data supports prescribing decisions for years to come.

### Step 02 — Interpret With Bioinformatics

An AI-assisted analysis layer processes the raw genomic data, flags clinically relevant variants, and structures them into standardized, prescribing-ready pharmacogenomic insights.

### Step 03 — Generate a Pharmacogenomic Passport

The result is a lifelong profile that consolidates each patient's medication-relevant genetics into a single, portable record clinicians can reference at the point of care.

### Step 04 — Integrate With Any Platform

Profiles surface across healthcare settings and systems — supporting consistent, evidence-based prescribing at both the individual and population level.

### Drug recommendations

#### Primary finding · current therapy

**Drug:** Nebivolol (CYP2D6 — poor metabolizer)  
**Source label:** FDA label · CPIC

- **Exposure:** CYP2D6 poor metabolizer → ~10–15× higher steady-state levels, higher bioavailability, longer half-life.
- **Clinical effect:** Trials show similar blood-pressure response and safety vs. normal metabolizers.
- **Label:** No mandatory dose adjustment required.

**Clinical suggestion:** Start at the lowest dose, titrate slowly to blood-pressure response, and monitor for bradycardia, hypotension, or fatigue. Review concurrent CYP2D6 inhibitors (e.g. paroxetine, fluoxetine, bupropion).

#### Other drugs

| Drug        | Gene    | Source                | Recommendation                                                                                       |
| ----------- | ------- | --------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Codeine     | CYP2D6  | CPIC level A · strong | Poor analgesia due to minimal morphine conversion. **Avoid — use a non-codeine analgesic.**          |
| Metoprolol  | CYP2D6  | DPWG                  | Raised plasma levels as an alternative β-blocker. **Use lowest effective dose; monitor heart rate.** |
| Clopidogrel | CYP2C19 | CPIC level A          | Reduced antiplatelet activation. **Prefer prasugrel or ticagrelor if an antiplatelet is needed.**    |
| Warfarin    | CYP2C9  | CPIC level A          | No CYP2C9-driven change. **Dose per standard algorithm (also consider VKORC1).**                     |

### Impact metric modals

#### Modal: 30% fewer clinically relevant adverse drug reactions

**Summary:** The PREPARE trial — a 12-gene panel implementation study across seven European countries (n=6,944) — showed genotype-guided prescribing reduced clinically relevant ADRs by 30% (OR 0.70).

**References:** Source 4

#### Modal: Nearly everyone carries an actionable PGx variant

**Summary:** A Swiss hospital-based cohort of 1,533 patients found 97.3% carried at least one clinically actionable pharmacogenetic variant relevant to prescribing.

**References:** Source 5

#### Modal: 1 in 4 prescriptions carry an actionable gene-drug interaction

**Summary:** In a nationwide analysis of 3.6M Dutch first prescriptions, 23.6% involved an actionable gene-drug interaction — approximately 1 in 4.

**References:** Source 6

#### Modal: 36% shorter hospitalisations

**Summary:** In the U-PGx PREPARE cost-utility analysis, mean hospitalisation duration fell from 2.37 days in the control group to 1.51 days in the PGx-guided group — a 36% reduction.

**References:** Source 7

#### Modal: ADR hospitalisations are expensive

**Summary:** The IATROSTAT-ECO study estimated each adverse-drug-reaction-related hospital admission in France costs ≈€5,974. The Lithuanian estimate of ≈€2,500 is a local adjustment of this EU benchmark.

**References:** Source 8

#### Modal: €5–10M estimated annual savings

**Summary:** Estimated annual savings for Lithuania's healthcare system, modeled from PREPARE trial outcomes and local adverse drug reaction incidence data.

**References:** Source 4

## Our goals (Vision)

**Eyebrow:** Our goals

**Headline:** Make genetics the backbone of every prescription — and grow from there into the medicine of the future.

**Intro:** We're building a system that uses genetic testing to help doctors make precise decisions — which drug, at what dose, and in what combination is safe for each patient. This reduces ineffective treatment, adverse reactions, and dosing errors — and, for the healthcare system, the cost of medications and the load on hospitals.

### Stage 1 · Core (2027–2028)

**Title:** AI recommendation platform

**Description:** Analyzes the patient's genetic data and, at the moment of prescribing, helps the doctor choose the right drug and dose — and flags dangerous drug combinations.

### Stage 2 (2028–2029)

**Title:** In-house laboratory

**Description:** A full cycle of genetic testing in Lithuania — from sample collection to interpretation and recommendations. Quality control at every step, fast turnaround, and sovereign data.

### Stage 3 (2029–2030)

**Title:** New fields and a scientific base

**Description:** Building on a working system and accumulated data — expansion into new areas of medicine, new tests, and joint research with universities and clinics.

### Why we do this

#### For patients

- Treatment matched to the individual genome
- The right drug and dose from the first prescription
- Fewer adverse reactions and ineffective treatments

#### For doctors

- A ready recommendation at the moment of prescribing
- No manual interpretation of genetics
- Lower risk, greater confidence in the decision

#### For the healthcare system

- Lower costs for complications and therapy selection
- Shorter hospital waiting times
- Citizens' data kept under control in Lithuania

## References (Sources 1–8)

All numbered citations used across the Problem and Impact sections.

### Source 1 — Haerdtlein et al., J Clin Med 2023

- **Title:** Which Adverse Events and Which Drugs Are Implicated in Drug-Related Hospital Admissions?
- **Detail:** Systematic review and meta-analysis of 12 studies on ADR-related admissions.
- **Key fact:** 8.3% prevalence — ≈1 in 12 admissions ADR-related
- **URL:** https://www.mdpi.com/2077-0383/12/4/1320

### Source 2 — Williams et al., Age Ageing 2025

- **Title:** Hospital admissions due to adverse drug reactions and adverse drug events in older adults: a systematic review
- **Detail:** Systematic review of ADR- and ADE-related hospital admissions in older adults.
- **Key fact:** 80% of medication-related admissions predictable or preventable
- **URL:** https://doi.org/10.1093/ageing/afaf231

### Source 3 — Institute of Hygiene Lithuania

- **Title:** Health Statistics of Lithuania 2023
- **Detail:** Official national inpatient episode volume from the Institute of Hygiene.
- **Key fact:** 560,362 annual hospital discharges
- **URL:** https://sveikstat.hi.lt/chart-summary-ctry.aspx?lang=eng&sel_rep_panel=8&top_loc=ctry&top_uid=110

### Source 4 — Swen et al., The Lancet 2023

- **Title:** A 12-gene pharmacogenetic panel to prevent adverse drug reactions (PREPARE trial)
- **Detail:** Open-label, multicentre, cluster-randomised implementation study across seven European countries (n=6,944).
- **Key fact:** 30% fewer clinically relevant ADRs (OR 0.70)
- **URL:** https://www.thelancet.com/journals/lancet/article/PIIS0140-6736(22)01841-4/abstract

### Source 5 — Hodel et al., Clin Transl Sci 2024

- **Title:** Prevalence of actionable pharmacogenetic variants and high-risk drug prescriptions: A Swiss hospital-based cohort study
- **Detail:** Analysis of 1,533 patients from a Swiss hospital biobank for clinically actionable pharmacogenes.
- **Key fact:** 97.3% carry ≥1 clinically actionable PGx variant
- **URL:** https://ascpt.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/cts.70009

### Source 6 — Bank et al., BMC Medicine 2019

- **Title:** Estimated nationwide impact of implementing a preemptive pharmacogenetic panel approach to guide drug prescribing in primary care in The Netherlands
- **Detail:** Nationwide analysis of 3,628,597 first prescriptions across ~95% of Dutch community pharmacies (2016).
- **Key fact:** 23.6% of new prescriptions carry an actionable gene-drug interaction
- **URL:** https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12916-019-1342-5

### Source 7 — U-PGx PREPARE cost-utility, eClinicalMedicine 2025

- **Title:** Multinational cost-utility analysis of panel-based pharmacogenetics-guided treatment of patients enrolled in the U-PGx PREPARE study
- **Detail:** Cost-utility analysis of 6,930 patients from the U-PGx PREPARE study across participating countries.
- **Key fact:** Hospital stay 2.37 → 1.51 days (−36%); €276 lower cost per actionable patient
- **URL:** https://www.thelancet.com/journals/eclinm/article/PIIS2589-5370(25)00620-0/fulltext

### Source 8 — Laroche et al., Br J Clin Pharmacol 2025

- **Title:** Economic burden of hospital admissions for adverse drug reactions in France: The IATROSTAT-ECO study
- **Detail:** Cost analysis of ADR-related hospital admissions in French public hospitals (payer perspective).
- **Key fact:** ≈€5,974 per ADR-related hospitalisation
- **URL:** https://bpspubs.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/bcp.16266
